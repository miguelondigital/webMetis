Métis Origins and the Making of a Beaded Identity

By Esmeralda Tellez

Introduction

May 28, 2025

Across what is now known as Western Canada, a unique group of people were born as a result of unions between the Indigenous and European worlds. The Métis people developed a strikingly beautiful expression of identity through their beadwork. Characterized by vivid floral and nature related designs with intricate craftsmanship. 1The significance of this craft is not only in its beauty, but in its role as a significant symbol for representation of identity, history, and resilience.

The Origins of Métis people

In around the 18th and 19th centuries the Métis people emerged as a result of unions between Indigenous women who were primary Anishinaabe, Cree, and Saulteaux–and European fur traders who were primary French and Scottish. According to the Otipemisiwak Métis Government, “The descendants of these marriages, the Métis, would form a distinct culture, collective consciousness, and strong Nationhood in the Northwest”. 2 These distinct communities were developed in the Red River Valley region with the Michif language being one of the primary languages they are characterized by. The Michif language is a mix of, “French nouns and Cree verbs and is spoken by the Métis in the provinces of Mantioba, Saskatchewan, Alberta, 3 British Columbia, Ontario, and in the South Slave Region of the Northwest” . This is only one of the many things which the Métis people are characterized by. As the community of the Métis grew, their need for identity and cultural identity also grew

Introduction of Beadwork

One of the most distinct forms of expression is beadwork, according to the Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada, the Métis people are often referred to as the “Flower Beadwork People” 4 due to their unique floral designs in their beadwork . Thanks to the introduction of European trade goods, especially glass beads, the Métis women began to incorporate these materials into their traditional designs. Before glass beads were used many Indigenous peoples used porcupine quillwork, natural pigments, and embroidery for decoration. With the introduction of glass beads, quills and other materials were replaced. These small beads allowed for more intricate, colorful, and durable designs. The Métis developed their unique motifs through learning from Grey Nuns. Through Grey nuns they were able to learn French silk embroidery. This form of embroidery had a heavy emphasis on delicate floral patterns which were very popular at the time . Giving the Métis people their particular and distinct form of floral beadwork

Beadwork as a Cultural Marker

Unlike the more geometric based beadwork styles of the Plains Indigenous peoples; the beadwork of the Métis are more nature based and emphasize the meaningful and close relationship to the land, nature, values, and journeys of the Métis. Beadwork has traditionally been kept alive through Métis women. Grandmothers and mothers pass down the art of beadwork to their granddaughters and daughters. Beadwork not only became a staple in Métis culture but also an important source of income for families along the river. The patterns are still popular 6 today as they were in the past 100 years .

Relevance

Today, beadwork continues to thrive not only as a cultural practice but also as a means of economic empowerment and contemporary artistic expression. There are many Métis individuals who set up their own businesses to sell their own beadwork or beadwork inspired art. Oftentimes on digital platforms such as Instagram, Etsy, and personal websites in order to reach a wider audience. For example Bead n’ Butter is a small business owned by Jessie Pruden. She started 7 her business during the COVID-19 Pandemic . Her work blends modern references and bold color palettes with traditional Métis beading techniques. Through her business she is highlighting the adaptability of Métis artistic traditions. She creates a bridge between older and new generations.

Businesses like Bread n’ Butter represent more than just entrepreneurship. These businesses are actions of cultural continuity and reclamation. Many contemporary beadworks today serve as a cultural marker and statement of Indigenous sovereignty. Depicting Métis artistry is still thriving today and continues to evolve.
